Martin Luther

The father of the Reformation
Martin Luther, painted by Lucas Cranach the Elder

Martin Luther began his religious life as an Augustinian monk on July 17, 1505, much to the chagrin of his father, who had hoped for him to become a lawyer. His decision to embrace the monastic life came after a dramatic incident in which he was almost struck by lightning on a return trip to the University of Erfurt, where he was a student. In fear of death, he cried out to Saint Anna and vowed to become a monk.

Luther did not find his life as a monk to be spiritually fulfilling. Rather, he struggled with despair that his confessions were not sufficient or complete enough:

I lost touch with Christ the Savior and Comforter, and made of him the jailer and hangman of my poor soul.
His superior, Johann von Staupitz, urged him to focus more on the merits of Christ and on changing his heart rather than on punishment and penance. Luther's struggle and this advice would later inform the development of his belief in sola fide, or justification by faith alone, which holds that it is only the faith in Jesus Christ which we receive through God's grace that contributes to our salvation, apart from any good works (such as penance) we might do.

The founding event of the reformation was Luther's posting of his Ninety-five Theses to the door of All Saints' Church in Wittenberg. The Theses addressed what he saw as corruptions in the Roman Catholic practice of indulgences, which claimed that punishment in Purgatory (for oneself or for another) could be reduced or even eliminated through an indulgence acquired through some good work. Often, these indulgences involved going on a pilgrimage or praying particular prayers, but some (especially in Luther's day) were connected to financial transactions such as donations to the reconstruction of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome. Luther questioned the efficacy and scriptural basis of this practice, which put him at odds with the Roman hierarchy.

After further confrontations with papal theologians regarding his doctrine on indulgences and justification by faith, Luther was excommunicated by Pope Leo X in 1521. Shortly after, he was called to account for his beliefs to the secular government of the Holy Roman Empire at the Diet of Worms (so named because it took place in the town of Worms, in modern-day Germany). Johann Eck, one of Luther's chief opponents, challenged him to consider his writings and recant what the Roman church viewed as heresy. Luther refused:

Unless I am convinced by the testimony of the Scriptures or by clear reason (for I do not trust either in the pope or in councils alone, since it is well known that they have often erred and contradicted themselves), I am bound by the Scriptures I have quoted and my conscience is captive to the Word of God. I cannot and will not recant anything, since it is neither safe nor right to go against conscience. May God help me. Amen.

Luther dedicated the rest of his life to developing his doctrine alongside other reformers such as Philip Melanchthon. His translation of the Bible into German allowed the common people to read the scriptures in their own language, and his publications and sermons formed the early basis of what is now known as Lutheranism.
